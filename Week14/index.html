The Final Result:

Turned out pretty good! As you can see below. I'm able to manipulated and "play" the (3) DC motors, (3) Solenoids, (2) Steppers, and (1) Servo from just one microcontroller and one power supply! Having just one microcontroller and one power supply was a big goal for this project to keep it small and able to travel. The second biggest was to get the circuits and motors all soldered into a permanent form. The third was to make it look nice. Right now, I think looks okay in this cardboard box but once I have the digital fabrication lab (3D printers, laser cutters) back at my disposal then I'll make a nicer container to encase this project.

Embedded youtube video of it working!

The Process

  1. I first assembled all the DC motors, solenoids, and servo with a UNO and breadboard. By using FET(s), I was able to get a fairly small form factor. For this step, I followed this tutorial here.


    2. - Then I assembled a stepper and h-bridge using an arduino nano


    - Next, powered each board up seperately and uploaded the "Standard Firmata" sketch from the Firmata library within the Arduino IDE. I talk about this a bit in a previous week here. Check out the "basic steps to get Max to talk to..."" here you'll find links to maxuino and the firmata library!.
    -Then once that is uploaded, I opened up Max MSP and intialized the boards and checked that each pin ran the motors as expected (they did).
    - Once I was confident it all worked seperately between these two boards I put them together within the same breadboard to share a single power supply!


  2. 2) Next big step was to make a protoboard and solder it all together then test it again. Once I pulled in the board and turned on the power suppy, all the motors turned on at full power then stopped abruptly. This is not expected or desired behavior. After some trouble shooting, we found that the first FET in the protoboard needed to be replaced. Once I desoldered that out and resoldered a new FET in. The whole project worked as expected again. I wanted to make the pins on the microcontroller feel more secure then the female headers could give me so I used a stacking protosheild for this board. I solders all the pin connections to this and then placed it on top of the Mega.



  3. 3) Now it was time to pull in my 49-key midi keyboard. This step was fairly simple. I identified the piano-keys I wanted to use (for example, C4/Middle C). I went through each motor and picked a key for it. Then I matched the midi number to each of these keys. In Max, I used a midiIn~ objects along with a Sel~ objects. This way I could easily identify the midi number that corresponded to the piano-key. Then send that to a motor that would only listen to it's assigned midi key and thus only that key could trigger the motor.


  4. 4) Play.
